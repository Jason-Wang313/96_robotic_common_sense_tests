\begin{abstract}
This paper is a submission-hardening audit, not an ICLR-main submission. The generated research bet was that robots should turn common-sense physical assumptions into executable affordance tests before committing to actions. We rebuilt the claim as a deterministic common-sense benchmark with five household-style tasks, seven physical assumption families, five distribution shifts, nine methods, seven seeds, ablations, stress sweeps, paired confidence intervals, and failure cases. The result is mixed but terminally negative. Executable tests beat non-human reasoning baselines under combined stress, but human-query policy reaches $0.633 \pm 0.007$ task success while the proposed method reaches $0.567 \pm 0.008$. The proposed method also has higher physical violations, higher damage, higher regret, high false rejection, and ablation contradictions. The terminal decision is therefore \textbf{KILL/ARCHIVE for ICLR main}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Terminal Decision}

The paper fails the ICLR-main evidence gate. The claim under test is not simply that physical common sense can be represented in text or geometry; that is already crowded by embodied reasoning, sequential affordance reasoning, large action models, LLM replanning, failure-reasoning data, robot common-sense embeddings, and model deliberation. The claim must be closed-loop: executable physical tests should change robot action choices and reduce failures without excessive cost or false rejection.

The local benchmark shows a useful partial-positive result: executable tests beat direct VLM action, LLM replanning, sequential 3D affordance reasoning, model-to-model deliberation, failure retrieval, and uncertainty-threshold probing under combined stress. However, they do not beat human-query policy. The paired proposed-minus-human success difference is $-0.06600 \pm 0.01009$ over 245 task/assumption/seed groups. Physical violation, damage, unsafe recall, and regret also favor the human-query baseline. We therefore archive the idea rather than submit it.

\section{Hostile Prior-Work Pressure}

The hostile literature pool includes Cosmos-Reason1-style embodied physical reasoning, SeqAfford, large action models with physical knowledge, human-in-the-loop LLM replanning, cross-environment failure reasoning for robotic manipulation, common-sense embeddings, physical reasoning benchmarks, affordance transfer, and safety deliberation. Under this prior-work pressure, a paper cannot claim novelty from ``common sense'' or ``affordance tests'' alone. The contribution would need to show that executable tests improve what the robot chooses and executes relative to these baselines. The v4 benchmark does not support that stronger claim.

\section{Benchmark}

The benchmark is a deterministic local simulator for physical-assumption testing and action selection. It is not robot hardware validation and is not high-fidelity physics. Its purpose is narrower: determine whether the proposed mechanism survives strong local baselines and stress tests.

\paragraph{Tasks.}
We evaluate stackable object placement, container liquid transfer, tool-use reachability, door/drawer opening, and cluttered navigation clearance.

\paragraph{Assumption families.}
The physical assumptions are support stability, containment/leakage, rigidity/deformation, tool reach/contact, articulation direction, clearance/collision, and mass/inertia.

\paragraph{Splits.}
We test nominal household scenes, visual ambiguity, counterintuitive physics, language-goal ambiguity, and combined common-sense stress.

\paragraph{Methods.}
The methods are direct VLM action policy, LLM common-sense replanner, sequential 3D affordance reasoner, uncertainty-threshold probe, model-to-model deliberation, failure retrieval policy, human-query policy, proposed executable common-sense tests, and oracle physical assumption.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Reasoning/test metrics are assumption diagnosis accuracy, unsafe-affordance rejection recall, false rejection, calibration error, and test informativeness. Closed-loop metrics are task success, physical violation rate, damage/spill/collision rate, test/query cost, human-query burden, and planning regret to oracle.

\section{Failure Cases}

The failure modes are task-specific. Liquid transfer still spills when containment tests are noisy. Stackable placement over-rejects valid placements under visual ambiguity. Cluttered navigation rejects unsafe paths but still has high violation in tight clearance. Door and drawer opening benefits from language and deliberation as much as executable tests. Tool-use reachability is often handled by geometry-only affordance reasoning without physical probes.

\section{Limitations}

This audit is honest about scope. The benchmark is deterministic and paper-specific, but it is not real robot validation, not high-fidelity physics, and not an integration of external VLM/LLM/affordance codebases. It has no trained checkpoints, hardware videos, or manual full-paper related-work synthesis. Those missing items would already prevent an ICLR-main submission. More importantly, the available local evidence is negative before reaching that bar.

\section{Conclusion}

Executable robotic common-sense tests are promising as an autonomous alternative to pure reasoning baselines, but the current method is not ICLR-main ready. It loses to human-query policy on success, physical violation, damage, unsafe recall, and regret, and its ablations contradict the full mechanism. The correct terminal action is \textbf{KILL/ARCHIVE}. A revival would require a substantially new empirical project where executable tests match or beat human-query recovery under a human-burden-aware objective in real robot or high-fidelity simulator experiments.
